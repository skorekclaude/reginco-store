# Cork, craft and a Catalan family: the story of Regincós

*An researched profile of Regincós (Tri.Pel, S.L.), the Spanish house behind the cork-handled professional hairbrush. Compiled from the brand's own publications, the Spanish business registry, a granted patent, hairdressing trade press and retail listings. Source confidence is marked throughout: **[verified]** = corroborated by independent sources, **[brand-claim]** = asserted by the company but not independently confirmed, **[single-source]** = found in only one place.*

---

## A workshop on the Costa Brava

Regincós is not a marketing invention. It is a small, real, family-run manufacturer on the north-east coast of Spain — in the province of **Girona, Catalonia**, around the towns of **Sant Feliu de Guíxols** and **Llagostera** on the Costa Brava. It trades under the legal name **Tri.Pel, S.L.** (tax ID CIF B17303231), an entity confirmed by three independent business registries (elEconomista/Empresite, eInforma, Kompass). It is a genuinely small operation — on the order of **seven employees** and roughly **€0.6–1.5M** in annual sales — which makes the global reach of its brushes all the more striking. **[verified]**

The story begins in **1962**, when **Ramón Regincós i Coronas** founded the firm. That founder and that year are not just brand copy: they are corroborated by independent Spanish hairdressing trade press, including a 2011 50th-anniversary feature in *Beauty Market* and a 2019 obituary in *Estetica Magazine España* — the obituary marking the death of the founder himself. **[verified]**

## The N1 — where it started

The first Regincós brush was the **"N1"**, made in 1962: an **oval cushion-pad brush with a plastic handle and nylon bristles**, the bristles set by hand into a **red rubber cushion**. It became a staple of 1960s Spanish salons and — remarkably — was reportedly still produced with the same hand-finishing process more than half a century later.

(A correction worth making: the N1 was *nylon-on-rubber*, not boar bristle. The boar-bristle reputation belongs to the brand's later professional ranges, not its first product.) **[single-source — brand history page]**

## 1987, and the idea that defined the house

The innovation Regincós is known for is the **"Cork Grip System"** — a hairbrush handle made of cork. The brand dates this to **1987** and describes it, on every one of its sites, as *"one of the most relevant innovations in the worldwide brush market."*

Here the careful researcher has to separate two things. The **1987 date is a brand-claim**: it is asserted consistently by Regincós (and repeated by retailers such as Jo Hansford), but it is not independently corroborated by a dated third-party record. What *is* independently documented is a **granted patent**: **WO1993016847A1, "Handle or grip made of cork for various objects,"** inventor **Ramón Regincos Dausa**, applicant **Tripel S L**, filed **28 February 1992** and published **2 September 1993**. So the cork-handle innovation is real and patented — but the public record puts the *patent* at 1992, five years after the brand's stated *development* date of 1987. Both can be true (you develop, then later patent), but it is worth naming the gap. **[verified — patent; brand-claim — 1987]**

Why cork? The brand's claims here are unusually well-supported by independent science. Cork is the outer bark (phellem) of the **cork oak, *Quercus suber***, a Mediterranean tree. The bark is stripped by hand **without felling the tree** — a living layer regenerates it — first at about 25 years of age and then every **9–12 years**, across a lifespan of **~200 years**. That makes cork genuinely renewable. Its physical properties also back the product story: cork is light, warm to the touch, **non-slip**, **antistatic**, thermally **insulating** and **chemically resistant** — exactly the qualities you want in a tool a stylist holds, wet-handed, all day. (One honest caveat: the dramatic carbon figures often quoted for cork trace back to cork-industry bodies like APCOR and Amorim, so treat the biggest numbers as industry-sourced.) **[verified — cork science]**

## Materials and the meaning of "handmade"

Regincós positions itself as **"Natural, sustainable, professional,"** with the older motto **"Quality & Prestige since 1962."** The "Made in Spain / artisanal" claim is concrete rather than decorative: brushes are described as **semi-hand-made** ("semifabricados") — assembled, polished and inspected by hand. The materials are consistent across sources:

- **Cork** for the signature grips;
- **Wood** (beech and other hardwoods) from **controlled/managed forest plantations**, finished with natural products to a warm "mahogany" tone;
- **100% pure, first-cut boar bristle**, often **reinforced with heat-resistant carbon-nylon** filaments to protect the hair and scalp (some models use a ~65% boar / 35% nylon mix);
- **Vegan / synthetic** bristle options for those who prefer them. **[verified across multiple retailers]**

## The business today — and a small mystery

The firm is run by the founder's **three sons — Ramón, Salvador and Marc Regincós**. Most sources (the company's own older site, the SBS distributor profile, the 2011 anniversary article) describe them as the **"second generation."** Curiously, the newer brand site, regincoshair.com, calls the company *"dirigida por la tercera generación"* — the **third generation**. The contradiction is unresolved: either a third generation has since joined the business, or the newer marketing copy is simply inconsistent. An honest profile leaves that open. **[conflicting sources]**

The product range has grown far beyond the N1: the **CORK** series (the versatile #640 / #650), **E.CORK** (eco / synthetic), a **WOOD** collection (FUSTA, METALLIC, 400'S, Twisted, Volumer), a **FLEX** line (YOGA, TOFFEE, POCKET), a **MEN** range (BARBA, MC Barber Shop), **COMBS**, and the sister **IBIZA HAIR** line — the latter reportedly created around 2000 with Aaron Johles and aimed squarely at American celebrity stylists, and home to the **VelChrome** thermal rollers. **[brand-claim / single-source]**

## Reputation and reach

For a seven-person workshop in Girona, the retail footprint is genuinely global. Regincós is sold:

- **London** — at the luxury salon-retailer **Jo Hansford** (the Cork Medium 640 at about £24.50); **[verified]**
- **New York** — at **Boyd's Madison Avenue** (Madison Ave & 61st), including blonde-bristle and men's vegan lines; **[verified]**
- across UK professional e-tailers — **Merritt's for Hair** (~16 SKUs, £17–30), **SBS Hair** (~19 items, £16.99–30), **Chris & Sons**; **[verified]**
- on **Amazon** marketplaces in the US, UK, DE, IE, ES, AE and beyond (the 16-row Cork Grip shows **4.4★ across 161 ratings** on Amazon.ie); **[single-source]**
- and via German (parfumdreams) and Spanish professional retailers (€13–33). **[single-source]**

Customer sentiment, where it can be read, is consistent: praise for **durability** ("very durable — lasting a long time"), **even blow-drying**, and gentleness on **sensitive scalps**; the most common complaint is **hair occasionally snagging at the tip** of round brushes during blow-drying. Pricing is mid-premium for a professional tool. **[single-source — verbatim review quotes]**

## What we know, and what we don't

**Solid:** the 1962 founding by Ramón Regincós i Coronas; the legal entity Tri.Pel, S.L. in Girona; the cork-handle patent (1992); the global premium-retail footprint; the materials and craft positioning.

**Brand-claim, uncorroborated:** the exact **1987** date for the Cork Grip, and the "most relevant innovation in the world brush market" framing.

**Unresolved:** second vs third generation; the precise workshop municipality (Sant Feliu de Guíxols vs Llagostera, both Girona); and the biggest cork-carbon statistics, which are industry-sourced.
