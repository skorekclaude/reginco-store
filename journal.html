Cork, craft and a Catalan family.

How a seven-person workshop on the Costa Brava — a family, a strip of cork bark, and sixty years of patience — became the quiet name behind the brushes in salons from London to New York.

Words from the Regincós Journal · June 2026 · 6 min

There is a particular weight to a Regincós brush. Pick one up and the first thing you notice is what you don't feel: no cold plastic, no slip. The handle is cork — warm, faintly soft, a little textured — and it sits in the hand as though it had been waiting for it. That handle is the whole story of the company. And the company is the story of one Catalan family that has been making brushes, by hand, since 1962.

A small workshop, a long memory

Regincós was not invented in a boardroom. It is a real, small manufacturer on the north-east coast of Spain — in Girona, on the Costa Brava, around the towns of Sant Feliu de Guíxols and Llagostera. It trades, unglamorously, as Tri.Pel, S.L. There are about seven people. The annual numbers are modest. And yet the brushes turn up on the styling stations of London's most exacting salons and on New York's Madison Avenue. It is the kind of disproportion you only get from making one thing, slowly, for a very long time.

The man who started it was Ramón Regincós i Coronas. The year was 1962. He died in 2019, and the Spanish hairdressing press — which had followed the house for decades — marked the loss. It is from those pages, and the public registry, that the firm can be read rather than merely repeated from its own brochures.

It began with the N1

His first brush had a name as plain as its ambition: the N1. An oval cushion pad, a plastic handle, nylon bristles pressed by hand into a bed of red rubber. Nothing exotic — but it was honest, and it was good, and in the salons of 1960s Spain that was enough. Remarkably, it is still made, more or less the same way, six decades on.

Make one thing, slowly, for sixty years, and the world eventually comes to your door.

The cork idea

The leap came in 1987. While the rest of the trade was reaching, as trades do, for cheaper plastic, Regincós reached instead for cork. The result — the Cork Grip System — is the thing the house is now known for: a handle that is light, warm, naturally non-slip and quietly indestructible. The company dates the idea to 1987; the patent that protects it was filed a little later, in 1992. Both can be true. You have the idea, and then one day you defend it.

Cork rewards the choice. It is the bark of the cork oak, Quercus suber, stripped from the living tree by hand every nine years or so and left to grow back — a harvest that fells nothing and repeats for two centuries. It is feather-light. It doesn't conduct heat. It shrugs off the chemistry of a working salon, and it never builds a charge of static. Everything you would want from a tool held, wet-handed, for ten hours a day. Long before "sustainable" was a word for a label, it was simply the better material.

Made by hand, and meant it

The rest is craft of the unhurried kind. Beech and other hardwoods from managed forests, brought to a warm mahogany by natural finishes. First-cut boar bristle, reinforced so it carries the hair's natural oils and protects the scalp rather than tearing at it — or a vegan bristle, for those who ask. Each brush assembled, polished and looked over by hand. "Quality and prestige since 1962" is the old motto, and it is the rare slogan the product simply confirms.

The family, still

The workshop is run now by Ramón's three sons — Ramón, Salvador and Marc. The newer pages hint at a third generation arriving; the family, in any case, has always been the point. The range has grown from that single N1 into a small library of tools — the Cork series, the eco-minded E.Cork, wood collections, men's brushes, combs, and the Ibiza Hair line made for American celebrity stylists — but the centre of gravity never moved. It is still a cork handle, still made in Girona, still finished by a human hand.

Is there a film?

Not the one you'd hope for — no patient documentary of sawdust and lamplight in the workshop. But the brushes are wherever stylists are: the official channel, a run of barbering demos like Fade, barbas y peinados, and the restless clip-life of Instagram. The proper "how it's made" film doesn't exist. Someone should make it.

What you are left with is unfashionably simple. A family. A material most people throw away as a stopper. And the patience to keep choosing it for sixty years. Pick up the brush again and it all makes sense — the warmth, the grip, the heft of something built to be kept. They don't make many like it. They never really did.
